## What is it?

Coercive control is when a partner or ex uses a **pattern of behaviour** to trap you, scare you, control what you do, where you go, and who you see. It's often invisible to the outside world. It can exist without physical violence. Many people don't recognise it as a crime because it builds up gradually — the "boiling frog" effect. If you feel like you're constantly walking on eggshells, that matters.

## Is this a crime?

**Yes.** Controlling and coercive behaviour has been a specific criminal offence since 2015. The Domestic Abuse Act 2021 strengthened the law and extended it to former partners.

## The law says...

Serious Crime Act 2015, Section 76 — it is an offence for a person repeatedly or continuously to engage in behaviour towards another person that is controlling or coercive, where the persons are personally connected, and the behaviour has a serious effect on the other person.

## To prove this, police need to show:

1. The suspect and the person affected are personally connected (intimate partners, ex-partners, or family members sharing a home)
2. The suspect engaged in **repeated or continuous** controlling or coercive behaviour
3. The behaviour had a **serious effect** on the person — either causing them to fear violence, or causing serious alarm or distress that substantially affected their day-to-day activities
4. The suspect knew or ought to have known the behaviour would have this effect

## Signs this may be happening to you

- Checking your phone, emails, or social media without permission
- Controlling money — stopping you working, giving you an "allowance," demanding receipts
- Isolating you from friends and family
- Constantly criticising or humiliating you
- Making all decisions without consulting you
- Monitoring your location using apps
- Threatening to share intimate images or private information
- Making you feel like you are "going mad" — denying things they said or did (gaslighting)
- Using children as a weapon — threats about custody or social services
- Controlling what you eat, wear, or how you present yourself
- Threatening to harm themselves if you leave

## What to do right now

1. **If you are in immediate danger, call 999**
2. **Call the National Domestic Abuse Helpline** (0808 2000 247) — free, 24/7, run by Refuge
3. **Keep a secret record** — dates, times, what happened (use this app's encrypted diary if safe to do so)
4. **Tell someone you trust** — a friend, family member, GP, or support worker
5. **You do not have to leave to get help** — support is available whatever you decide

## Maximum sentence

**5 years imprisonment** (increased from the original 2015 maximum by the Domestic Abuse Act 2021)

## Common myths — the truth

| Myth | Truth |
|------|-------|
| "It's not abuse if they don't hit me" | Coercive control is a crime — physical violence is not required |
| "They only do it because they love me" | Control is not love — healthy relationships are based on respect and freedom |
| "It's not that bad — other people have it worse" | If their behaviour is affecting your daily life, it is serious and it is a crime |

## Note on the Domestic Abuse Act 2021

The Act created a statutory definition of domestic abuse that includes psychological, emotional, economic and coercive abuse. It also extended coercive control to former partners who no longer share a home.
