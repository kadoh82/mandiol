🔴 Rape

Sexual Offences Act 2003, Section 1 — Maximum: Life imprisonment

🌿 In plain English

Rape is when someone puts their penis into your vagina, anus, or mouth without your agreement. It doesn't matter if it's a stranger, a partner, or an ex. You don't have to have said "no" out loud — if you didn't freely agree, it's rape. Freezing is a recognised trauma response (fight, flight, or freeze). Freezing is NOT giving consent.

⚖️ Points to Prove

1

The suspect intentionally penetrated the victim's vagina, anus or mouth with their penis.

2

The victim did not consent to the penetration.

3

The suspect did not reasonably believe the victim consented.

Stranger Rape — Important

A stranger rape is where the suspect is unknown to the victim or a very recent acquaintance. Police are more likely to arrest immediately in stranger rape because there is a risk to the wider public (the suspect may offend again). Forensic investigation is treated as urgent.

Consent

A person consents if they agree by choice and have the freedom and capacity to make that choice. You cannot consent if asleep, unconscious, severely intoxicated, threatened, or under 16.

Common Myths

  • "Freezing means I consented" — False. Freeze is a neurological trauma response.
  • "My partner can't rape me" — Marital rape has been illegal since 1991 (R v R).
  • "I was drunk so it's my fault" — Intoxication is the perpetrator's problem, not yours.
  • "It's too late to report" — There is no time limit for rape in England & Wales.

🔴 Sexual Assault by Penetration

Sexual Offences Act 2003, Section 2 — Maximum: Life imprisonment

🌿 In plain English

This covers penetration of the vagina or anus with a body part other than a penis (such as fingers or tongue) or with an object, without your consent. It carries the same maximum sentence as rape and is treated with equal seriousness by police and courts.

⚖️ Points to Prove

1

The suspect intentionally penetrated the vagina or anus of the victim with a part of their body or anything else.

2

The penetration was sexual in nature.

3

The victim did not consent.

4

The suspect did not reasonably believe the victim consented.

Examples

  • Penetration with fingers without consent
  • Penetration with an object without consent
  • Oral penetration not involving a penis

🔴 Sexual Assault

Sexual Offences Act 2003, Section 3 — Maximum: 10 years imprisonment

🌿 In plain English

Sexual assault is any intentional sexual touching without your consent. This includes groping, grabbing, unwanted kissing, rubbing against you in a crowd, touching over clothing, or forcing you to touch someone else. No penetration is needed. Any unwanted sexual touch counts.

⚖️ Points to Prove

1

The suspect intentionally touched the victim.

2

The touching was sexual.

3

The victim did not consent.

4

The suspect did not reasonably believe the victim consented.

Examples

  • Groping on a night out or public transport
  • Unwanted kissing
  • Colleague touching your body at work without permission
  • Rubbing against you in a crowd for sexual gratification

🟠 Sexual Harassment

Criminal Justice Act 2023 (street) / Equality Act 2010 (workplace)

🌿 In plain English

Sexual harassment is unwanted behaviour of a sexual nature that causes you distress. Since 2023, street sexual harassment is a criminal offence. At work, your employer has a legal duty to protect you. You don't have to have said "stop" — if it was unwanted and sexual, it qualifies.

⚖️ Points to Prove (Street)

1

The behaviour was unwanted by the victim.

2

The behaviour was of a sexual nature.

3

It occurred in a public place.

4

The victim was alarmed, distressed or humiliated (or this was intended).

Examples

  • Catcalling, wolf-whistling, sexual comments in the street
  • Following while making sexual remarks
  • Sending unsolicited intimate images online
  • Unwanted touching or comments at work

👁️ Voyeurism & Upskirting

SOA 2003 s.67 / Voyeurism (Offences) Act 2019

🌿 In plain English

Voyeurism is secretly watching or recording someone in a private situation. Upskirting (photographing under someone's clothing) became a specific criminal offence in 2019. Hidden cameras in bathrooms or bedrooms are also covered. You have a right to privacy — always.

⚖️ Points to Prove (Upskirting)

1

The suspect operated equipment beneath a person's clothing.

2

Without that person's consent.

3

To observe genitalia/buttocks, obtain sexual gratification, OR to humiliate/distress.

Found a hidden camera? Do this:

  1. Do NOT touch it
  2. Photograph it in place
  3. Call police immediately (101 or 999)
  4. Note exact location and time

🟡 Controlling & Coercive Behaviour

Serious Crime Act 2015, s.76 — Maximum: 5 years imprisonment

🌿 In plain English

Coercive control is when a partner or ex uses a pattern of behaviour to trap, frighten, or control you. It doesn't require physical violence. Many women don't recognise it as a crime because it builds up gradually. If you feel like you're constantly "walking on eggshells" — that matters. It is a crime.

⚖️ Points to Prove

1

The suspect and victim are personally connected (intimate partner, ex, or family in shared home).

2

The suspect engaged in repeated or continuous controlling or coercive behaviour.

3

The behaviour had a serious effect: fear of violence, OR serious alarm/distress affecting daily life.

4

The suspect knew or ought to have known the behaviour would have this effect.

Signs — Is This Happening to You?

  • Checking your phone or emails without permission
  • Controlling your money or stopping you from working
  • Isolating you from friends and family
  • Constantly criticising or humiliating you
  • Monitoring your location
  • Making you feel like you're "going mad" (gaslighting)
  • Using children as a weapon
  • Threatening to share your private photos or information

🟣 Revenge Porn

Online Safety Act 2023 — Maximum: 2 years imprisonment

🌿 In plain English

Sharing or threatening to share intimate images of you without your consent is a criminal offence. It doesn't matter if you originally took the photo yourself or shared it with a partner. Even threatening to share is now illegal under the 2023 Act. Act fast — evidence and image removal are time-sensitive.

⚖️ Points to Prove

1

A photograph or film was shared (or threatened to be shared).

2

It showed private sexual activity or genitalia.

3

The victim did not consent to the sharing.

4

Intent to cause distress (or recklessness as to causing it).

Do This Immediately

  • Screenshot the post, the URL, the account — preserve everything
  • Call Revenge Porn Helpline: 0345 6000 459
  • Visit StopNCII.org to create a digital fingerprint
  • Report to police
  • Report directly to the platform

🟣 Intimate Deepfakes

Online Safety Act 2023 / Criminal Justice Bill 2024

🌿 In plain English

A deepfake uses AI to put your face onto another body, often in sexual situations. This can be done using ordinary photos from your social media — you don't have to have posed for anything. Sharing intimate deepfakes without consent is already illegal. The 2024 Bill will also criminalise creating them, even if never shared.

⚖️ Points to Prove (Sharing)

1

A deepfake intimate image of the victim was shared.

2

The victim did not consent.

3

No reasonable belief that the victim consented.

What to Do

  • Screenshot all evidence before reporting (it may be deleted)
  • Report to police with all evidence
  • Contact Revenge Porn Helpline — they handle deepfakes too
  • Contact a solicitor for an emergency injunction

Securing Your Evidence

Good evidence can make a crucial difference. Here's what to preserve and how.

⚠️

Do not delete anything

Never delete messages, photos, or emails — even distressing ones. Altering evidence can damage a case. Preserve first, seek support second.

📱 Digital & Phone Evidence

▾
💬

Text messages & WhatsApp

Screenshot full conversations including the contact name/number. On WhatsApp: tap ⋮ > More > Export Chat. Back up to secure cloud (not just your phone).

📧

Emails

Forward threatening emails to a secure account. Screenshot showing sender address and full date clearly.

📱

Social media

Screenshot profile URL + abusive post + comments. Download content from the platform before reporting — posts may be deleted after reporting.

🎙️

Voicemails

Do NOT delete threatening voicemails. Write down date, time and content. Police can retrieve deleted voicemails in some cases.

📍

Tracking apps / stalkerware

If you think your phone is monitored, do NOT search for this on your device. Use a trusted friend's phone. Call the DA Helpline before changing any settings — they can advise safely.

📹

CCTV footage

CCTV is typically overwritten within 28–31 days. If an incident happened in public, report to police quickly so footage can be urgently requested.

🩹 Physical Evidence

▾
🏥

Forensic medical (SARC)

For sexual offences, attend a SARC as soon as possible. Ideally within 72 hours. Do not wash, shower, or change clothes beforehand if you can help it. A SARC can preserve forensic evidence even if you're not ready to report to police.

📸

Injuries

Photograph injuries with a ruler or coin for scale. Date and time-stamp photos. Visit GP or A&E so injuries are documented in medical notes.

🧥

Clothing

Do not wash clothing worn during an assault. Place in a paper bag (NOT plastic). Store somewhere safe outside the home if possible.

📓

Keep a diary

For ongoing offences (coercive control, harassment), write dates, times, what happened, what was said. Date and sign each entry. This has real evidential value.

⏱️ Forensic Windows

▾
🔬

Rape (vaginal)

Up to 7 days — but ideally within 72 hours for best evidence. DNA degrades quickly.

🔬

Rape (anal or oral)

2–3 days for anal, up to 48 hours for oral penetration. Shorter forensic windows.

🔬

Sexual assault (skin contact)

Up to 48 hours. Touch DNA (from fingers/hands on skin) is possible but degrades quickly.

🔬

Drug/alcohol facilitation

Urine within 72–96 hours. Hair testing for GHB/ketamine etc. can go back up to 3 months.

💡

Even outside the window

Always attend. Your account, injuries, and emotional state can still be documented. It is NEVER too late to speak to a SARC.

The Police Investigation Process

Understanding your options — step by step.

1

999 — Emergency

Call 999 if you are in immediate danger, the offender is nearby, or there is ongoing risk to you or anyone else.

2

101 — Non-Emergency

To report a crime that has already happened with no immediate danger. Also available: online reporting via your local force's website.

3

In person at a police station

You can attend any police station — it does not have to be nearest to where it happened. Ask for a same-sex officer — you are entitled to make this request.

4

Via a SARC or ISVA

A Sexual Assault Referral Centre or Independent Sexual Violence Adviser can help you make a report, or report alongside you, if you prefer not to go alone.

⭐ "Telling, Not Reporting" — You Are In Control

You can give information to police without making a formal report and without triggering an arrest.

✓

Your account goes on record.

✓

Helps police identify if this person has offended before.

✓

Can be used as "bad character" evidence in future prosecutions.

✓

Police will NOT arrest without your knowledge and consent.

✓

You remain in control. You can move to a formal report at any time.

Many offenders are repeat offenders. Each report — even without a formal complaint — builds a picture. You may be the fifth person to report the same individual.

💚 Self-Referral — No Police Required

You can attend a SARC without any police involvement at all. Your samples are stored securely. No report is made to police unless you want one. If you later decide to report, your preserved evidence is available.

1

Arrive at the SARC

With or without police. Met by a trained sexual offences nurse examiner (SONE). You are in a comfortable, private facility — not a police station.

2

Initial conversation

A general account of what happened. Detailed evidence gathering happens at your ABE interview later. Who is present: the nurse/examiner, an ISVA if available, a supporter of your choice.

3

Forensic medical examination (your choice)

You can decline any part. Samples may include: vaginal/anal/oral swabs, fingernail scrapings, hair combings, clothing, blood (toxicology), urine. You can request a female examiner.

4

Healthcare

Emergency contraception, STI testing, HIV PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) if needed. All offered as part of SARC care.

5

Referral to ISVA

An Independent Sexual Violence Adviser provides free, ongoing support however you proceed — whether you report or not.

To find your nearest SARC: nhs.uk/live-well/sexual-health/help-after-rape or call Rape Crisis on 0808 802 9999.
1

Written Statement (MG11)

Formal written account taken by an officer. You can take breaks, have an ISVA present, and read it before signing. You can request a copy at any time.

2

ABE Video Interview — Achieving Best Evidence

For sexual offences, police offer a video-recorded interview instead of a written statement.

The room: A comfortable civilian room with sofas — not an interview suite. Private and sound-insulated.

Who is present:
• A trained detective (sexual offence specialist)
• Sometimes an intermediary — a registered communication specialist (used if you have communication needs, autism, learning difficulties, or severe trauma response)
• Your ISVA can be present (to support, not to speak during interview)
• A trusted friend, family member, or appropriate adult may attend — ask your officer
3

The interview process

1. Officer explains format. 2. You tell what happened in your own words (free narrative). 3. Officer asks open, clarifying questions. 4. You can ask for a break at any point. 5. Everything is recorded (audio and video).

4

Why the ABE matters

The recording is played as your evidence-in-chief at trial. You do not stand up in open court and re-tell everything. You will still be cross-examined — but via video link with special measures.

1

Voluntary Interview

Police may invite the suspect to attend voluntarily. They can bring a solicitor. They are not obliged to attend — police may then arrest. The caution still applies and the interview is still recorded.

2

Arrest

Police arrest if they have reasonable grounds to suspect the offence and arrest is necessary. In stranger rape cases, police are more likely to arrest immediately due to ongoing public risk. After arrest: suspect is taken to custody, detained up to 24 hours (extendable), entitled to free legal advice.

3

Intimate samples from the suspect

If the suspect is arrested within 72 hours of the offence, police can take intimate samples from them (mouth swabs, genital swabs, fingernail scrapings). This is why swift reporting and the forensic window matter so much.

4

CPS charging decision

Police pass the file to the Crown Prosecution Service. CPS apply: (1) sufficient evidence for a realistic prospect of conviction, (2) prosecution in the public interest. You will be told the outcome with reasons.

If CPS decide not to charge, request a review under the Victims' Right to Review scheme.
Bail With Conditions

Released — with restrictions

Suspect released with a date to return. Conditions may include: no contact with you (direct or indirect), exclusion from specified areas (near your home/work), surrender of passport, regular reporting to police station.

Tell your officer what conditions YOU need. You have the right to provide input. Breach of bail conditions is a criminal offence.

Bail Without Conditions

Released — no restrictions

Suspect released with a return date but no conditions. Less common in VAWG cases. If you are concerned about risk, raise this with your officer and ISVA — bail conditions can be applied later.

Released Under Investigation (RUI)

No date set — investigation continues

No bail date, no conditions. The suspect is free while investigation continues. This can feel unsettling. You can ask police about their timeline and what conditions might be available. Your ISVA can advocate for you.

Remanded Into Custody

Kept in prison until court

Applied where there is: flight risk, risk of reoffending, prior breaches of bail, or a serious offence with strong evidence. Granted by a court, not police. You can ask to be notified if they are released.

The Court Process

What happens if the case goes to trial — and what you're entitled to.

68%

Conviction rate for rape cases that reach Crown Court trial

CPS data 2023/24. If your case reaches trial, it is more likely to result in conviction than not. Reporting matters.

💜

If you don't report, they will do it again

Research consistently shows that VAWG offenders are repeat offenders. Every report — even "telling not reporting" — protects other women. Your voice matters. You will be believed. You are not alone in this.

1

Plea Hearing

Serious offences (rape, sexual assault) go to Crown Court. The suspect enters a plea — guilty or not guilty.

2

Trial (if not guilty plea)

A jury of 12 members of the public hears the evidence. Your ABE video interview is played — you do not re-tell everything in open court. A prosecution barrister asks follow-up questions. The defence barrister cross-examines you.

3

Special Measures — Your Entitlement

As a victim of a sexual or violent crime, you are automatically entitled to ask for:

🛡️ Screen in courtroom — hidden from the defendant
📺 Video link — give evidence from a separate room
👤 Supporter present while you give evidence
🚪 Public excluded from court (reporting restrictions)
🎥 Section 28 — pre-recorded cross-examination (available in all Crown Courts from 2022): your cross-examination is recorded before trial — you don't have to attend on the trial day at all
4

Cross-Examination Protections

The defence cannot ask about your sexual history without leave of the court (Youth Justice and Criminal Evidence Act 1999, s.41). The judge decides whether such questions are genuinely relevant — they exist to test evidence, not to shame you.

5

Victim Personal Statement (VPS)

You can write a statement describing the impact of the crime on your life. It is read to the court at sentencing. You can read it yourself, have the prosecutor read it, or submit it in writing. The judge must consider it. Your voice matters here too.

6

Verdict & Sentencing

The jury delivers a verdict. If guilty, the judge passes sentence. If you are unhappy with the sentence, you can ask the Solicitor General to refer it to the Court of Appeal if it appears unduly lenient (Unduly Lenient Sentence scheme — 28 days from sentencing).

Civil Protection Routes

You don't have to wait for a criminal prosecution. Civil law can protect you — sometimes within 24 hours.

Family Law Act 1996

Non-Molestation Order

Forbids the abuser from using or threatening violence, harassing, pestering, or contacting you by any means.

How fast: Emergency order within 24 hours (ex parte — without the abuser present).

Who can apply: Current/former partner, family member, someone you have a child with.

Legal Aid: Available with evidence of domestic abuse. Many solicitors offer free initial consultation.

Breach: Criminal offence — up to 5 years imprisonment. Call 999 immediately.

Family Law Act 1996

Occupation Order

Removes the abuser from the home — even if it's in their name. Can exclude them from the surrounding area.

How fast: Emergency order within 24–48 hours. Duration: initially 6–12 months, extendable.

Crime & Security Act 2010

Domestic Violence Protection Order (DVPO)

Police apply for this — no involvement required from you. Removes the abuser from your home for up to 28 days. Gives you breathing space to make decisions.

How fast: Applied for by police within 48 hours of an incident.

Protection from Harassment Act 1997

Civil Injunction

If harassed on two or more occasions, you can apply for a civil injunction without a criminal conviction. Also: a criminal court can impose a Restraining Order on conviction — or even on acquittal.

CICA Scheme

Criminal Injuries Compensation

Financial compensation for victims of violent crime — regardless of whether there was a prosecution. Apply at gov.uk/claim-compensation-criminal-injury

Time limit: Generally 2 years from the offence. Exceptions for historic cases. Ranges from £1,000 to over £500,000.

Online Safety Act 2023

Emergency Image Removal Injunction

A solicitor can apply for an emergency injunction requiring a platform to remove intimate images or deepfakes immediately. Can be done within hours in urgent cases.

Clare's Law & Disclosure Schemes

Your right to know if a partner has a history of violence or abuse.

Domestic Violence Disclosure Scheme — 2014

Clare's Law — Right to Ask

In 2009, Clare Wood was murdered by her partner George Appleton, who had a known history of violence against women. Her father campaigned for a law to give women the right to ask police about a partner's history. Clare's Law was introduced nationally in England & Wales in 2014.

Right to Ask: You can ask your local police whether a current partner has a history of domestic abuse or violence. Police investigate and, if there is relevant information and you are assessed to be at risk, they will tell you.

Right to Know: Police can also proactively tell you — without you asking — if they believe you are at risk from a partner.

How to apply: Contact your local police station or call 101. Ask to make a DVDS (Domestic Violence Disclosure Scheme) enquiry. Usually processed within 35 days.

Available in: England & Wales (2014), Scotland (2015), Northern Ireland (2022).

Child Sex Offender Disclosure Scheme

Sarah's Law — Right to Ask About Children

Allows parents, carers or guardians to ask police whether a person who has contact with their child has been convicted of child sexual offences. Contact your local police to make an enquiry.

Disclosure Schemes

Stalking Protection Orders

Since 2020, police can apply for a Stalking Protection Order (SPO) as a civil measure to protect victims of stalking before a criminal prosecution. It can prohibit specific behaviour and require the stalker to attend an intervention programme. Contact the Suzy Lamplugh Trust National Stalking Helpline: 0808 802 0300.

It Is Never Too Late

There is no time limit for reporting rape or serious sexual offences in England & Wales.

💚 The Law

Unlike most criminal offences, there is no statutory limitation period for rape and serious sexual offences in England & Wales. You can report an offence that happened 5, 10, 30 or 40 years ago. Historic reports are taken seriously by police, prosecutors, and courts.

💜

Why people don't report at the time — and why that's completely understandable

Shock, shame, fear, not recognising it as a crime, cultural pressure, being very young, alcohol involved, trust in the abuser. None of these mean you cannot report now. Delay is well understood by police and courts.

Real Case Law Examples

Historic prosecutions that resulted in justice

2013

Stuart Hall

TV presenter convicted of 14 indecent assaults on girls from 1967–1985. Many victims came forward decades later. Sentenced to 30 months. His case encouraged many others to come forward and was a landmark moment for historic sexual offence prosecution.

2014

Rolf Harris

Convicted of 12 indecent assaults between 1968 and 1986. Victims had not reported at the time. Sentenced to 5 years 9 months. Court of Appeal dismissed appeals. Case showed that a high-profile perpetrator with no prior record can still be convicted on historic complaints.

Ongoing

Operation Hydrant

National police coordination of historic non-recent child sexual abuse investigations. As of 2023, has investigated thousands of suspects including public figures. Demonstrates police capacity and commitment to historic cases at scale.

Key Case Law

R v Barker [2010] EWCA Crim 4

Court of Appeal affirmed that delay in reporting does not automatically undermine a complainant's reliability. Jury directions are given to this effect in all Crown Court sexual offence trials.

Important Principle

The Power of Multiple Complainants

When one person reports, police may find other victims. The pattern of behaviour becomes the prosecution's strongest evidence. In Hall and Harris cases, the first report triggered others — and justice followed. Your report may be the one that makes the difference.
