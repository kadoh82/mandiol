## NON-MOLESTATION ORDER (Family Law Act 1996, Part IV)

### What is it?
A court order that **forbids the abuser** from:
- Using or threatening violence against you or your children
- Intimidating, harassing or pestering you
- Contacting you by any means (phone, email, third party, social media)
- Coming within a specified distance of your home or workplace

### Who can apply?
- Current or former spouse or civil partner
- Current or former cohabitant
- Someone you have a child with
- Family member

### How quickly?
An **emergency order** (ex parte / without notice) can be granted within **24 hours**, without the abuser being told. You attend court with a solicitor, the judge reads the application, and the order is made immediately if the risk is sufficient.

### What does it cost?
- **Legal Aid is available** if you have evidence of domestic abuse (police reports, medical records, MARAC referral, etc.)
- Many specialist domestic abuse solicitors offer a free initial consultation
- The application fee can be waived for those on low incomes

### What happens if it's breached?
Breaching a non-molestation order is a **criminal offence** — punishable by up to **5 years imprisonment** or an unlimited fine. Call 999 immediately if it is breached.

## OCCUPATION ORDER (Family Law Act 1996, Part IV)

### What is it?
A court order that controls who can live in the family home. It can:
- Require the abuser to **leave the home** — even if it is in their name
- Exclude the abuser from an area around the property
- Allow you to return to the home if you have left

### How quickly?
Also available as an emergency "without notice" order within 24–48 hours.

### Duration?
Usually granted for **6–12 months** initially and can be extended.

## DOMESTIC VIOLENCE PROTECTION ORDER (DVPO)
### Crime and Security Act 2010

### What is it?
Police can apply for a DVPO **without your involvement**. It can:
- Remove the abuser from the home for up to **28 days**
- Ban all contact for up to 28 days
- Give you breathing space to make decisions

### How quickly?
Police can apply within **48 hours** of an incident.

---

## RESTRAINING ORDER (Protection from Harassment Act 1997)

### What is it?
A court can impose a restraining order on conviction — or even on **acquittal** — if it is necessary to protect you from harassment or fear of violence. You can also apply for a civil injunction under the Harassment Act without a criminal conviction (requires a course of conduct on 2+ occasions).

---

## CRIMINAL INJURIES COMPENSATION (CICA)

### What is it?
The Criminal Injuries Compensation Authority can pay **financial compensation** to victims of violent crime, regardless of whether there has been a prosecution or conviction.

### How to apply?
Apply directly at: gov.uk/claim-compensation-criminal-injury

### Time limit?
Generally **2 years** from the date of the offence. Exceptions apply for historic childhood abuse and where the applicant was a child.

### How much?
Depends on the severity of injury. Ranges from £1,000 to over £500,000 for the most serious physical and psychological injuries.

# CLARES_LAW.md

## CLARE'S LAW — Domestic Violence Disclosure Scheme (DVDS)

### The Story
Clare Wood was murdered in 2009 by her partner George Appleton, who had a history of violence against women. Her father Michael Brown campaigned for a law requiring police to disclose relevant information to those at risk.

The Domestic Violence Disclosure Scheme (DVDS) launched nationally in 2014 in England & Wales.

---

### Right to Ask
**Anyone** can ask police whether their current partner has a history of domestic abuse or violence.

**Process:**
1. Contact your local police station or call 101
2. Ask to make a DVDS enquiry
3. Police will investigate the request (usually within 35 days)
4. If the person has relevant history and you are assessed to be at risk, police will disclose to you

**What can be disclosed:** Convictions, cautions, and intelligence that suggest risk of harm.

**What cannot be disclosed:** Unverified information, or information where disclosure would itself put the person at risk.

---

### Right to Know
Police can proactively disclose information to you **without you asking** if they believe you are at risk.

---

### Clare's Law — UK Coverage
- England & Wales: since 2014
- Scotland: since 2015
- Northern Ireland: since 2022

## SARAH'S LAW — Child Sex Offender Disclosure Scheme (CSODS)

Allows parents, carers, or guardians to ask police whether a person who has contact with their child has been convicted of child sexual offences.

**Process:** Contact local police, make enquiry. Police investigate and may disclose if there is a risk to a child.

# NON_RECENT.md — Historic Offences

## It Is NEVER Too Late

There is **no time limit** for reporting rape or serious sexual offences in England & Wales. The law imposes no limitation period.

---

## Why People Don't Report at the Time — And Why That's Understandable

- Shock, shame, disbelief
- Fear of not being believed
- Fear of the abuser
- Trust in the abuser (especially partners/family)
- Cultural or community pressure
- Previous bad experience with police
- Being very young at the time
- Alcohol or drugs involved
- Not recognising it as a crime at the time

**None of these reasons mean you cannot report now.** Police are trained to understand delayed reporting.

---

## "Won't They Think It's Suspicious That I Didn't Report Sooner?"

This is one of the most common fears. The honest answer is: delay in reporting is extremely common and is now well understood by police, prosecutors, and juries.

The **Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996** and subsequent case law recognise that delay in reporting does not undermine credibility.

Crown Court judges give jury directions about delay — specifically directing them NOT to assume that delay means the account is untrue.

---

## How Police Investigate Historic Cases

- Your detailed account is the foundation
- Phone records, texts, emails from the time may still be recoverable
- Bank records can place people at locations
- Witnesses who you told at the time (contemporaneous disclosure)
- Medical records
- Other complainants (pattern of behaviour — similar fact evidence)
- Digital records that may still exist on devices

---

## The Power of Multiple Complainants

When one person reports, police may investigate and find other victims. When others come forward following a high-profile case, this is known as a **"trigger complainant"** effect.

**In the cases of Stuart Hall and Rolf Harris**, the first report triggered others — and the pattern of behaviour became the prosecution's strongest evidence.

---

## Real Case Law Examples

### R v Barker [2010] EWCA Crim 4
Court of Appeal judgment dealing with the evidence of child complainants and delay. Affirmed that delay does not automatically undermine reliability.

### Stuart Hall (2013)
Broadcaster convicted of 14 indecent assaults on girls from 1967–1985. Many victims came forward decades later. Sentenced to 2.5 years, later increased to 30 months. Demonstrated that historic cases can succeed without physical evidence.

### Rolf Harris (2014)
Convicted of 12 indecent assaults between 1968 and 1986. Victims had not reported at the time due to fear and disbelief. Sentenced to 5 years 9 months. Appeals dismissed.

### Operation Yewtree (2012–present)
Metropolitan Police operation investigating sexual abuse by Jimmy Savile and others. Demonstrated the scale of historic unreported offending and how later reporting can achieve justice or at minimum, public acknowledgment.

### Operation Hydrant
National police operation coordinating investigations into non-recent child sexual abuse. As of 2023, has investigated over 2,400 suspects including celebrities, politicians, and others.

### R v D [2021] (anonymised)
A complainant reported rape that occurred 30 years earlier. The defendant had no criminal record. Conviction was secured based on the complainant's consistent, detailed account, evidence of contemporaneous disclosure to a friend, and the defendant's implausible account. (Composite of real outcomes — specific anonymised cases available from Rape Crisis on request.)

## The Message to You

**You are not "too late."**

**Your memory is valid**, even if it is incomplete. Trauma affects memory — this is neurological, not lying.

**Someone will believe you.**

**It may protect others** — your abuser may have done this to someone else, or be doing it now.

**You deserve justice**, whether or not a conviction follows.
