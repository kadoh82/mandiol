## RAPE — Sexual Offences Act 2003, Section 1

### Plain English
Rape is when someone puts their penis into your vagina, anus, or mouth without your agreement. It can be a stranger, a partner, an ex, or anyone else — it is still rape. You do not have to have said "no" out loud. If you didn't freely agree — or couldn't agree — it's rape. Freezing (the body's "freeze response") is a recognised reaction to threat. Freezing is NOT consent.

### Is this a crime? YES.

### Points to Prove
1. The suspect intentionally penetrated the vagina, anus or mouth of another person with their penis
2. The complainant did not consent to the penetration
3. The suspect did not reasonably believe the complainant consented

### What is consent?
A person consents if they agree by **choice** and have the **freedom** and **capacity** to make that choice.

You **cannot** legally consent if you are:
- Asleep or unconscious
- So drunk or drugged you don't know what's happening
- Being threatened or forced
- Under 16 (the age of consent in England & Wales)
- Deceived about the nature of the act or identity of the person

### Stranger Rape — Definition
A stranger rape is where the perpetrator is unknown to the victim, or is someone met very recently (e.g. same night). This is significant because:
- Police may arrest immediately due to risk to the wider public (the suspect may offend again)
- Forensic investigation is treated as urgent
- DNA databases may be searched
- The police response timeline is typically faster

### Myths vs Truth
| Myth | Truth |
|------|-------|
| "If I didn't fight back, it wasn't rape" | Freezing is a trauma response — it is not consent |
| "My partner can't rape me" | Marital rape has been illegal since R v R [1991] UKHL 12 |
| "I was drunk so it's my fault" | Intoxication does not diminish the offender's guilt |
| "What I wore was an invitation" | Clothing never implies consent |
| "I didn't report at the time so it's too late" | There is no time limit for reporting rape |

### Maximum Sentence: Life imprisonment

### Key Case Law
- **R v R [1991]** — established that a husband can rape his wife
- **R v Bree [2007]** — intoxication and capacity to consent
- **R v Olugboja [1982]** — submission is not the same as consent

## SEXUAL ASSAULT BY PENETRATION — Sexual Offences Act 2003, Section 2

### Plain English
This offence covers penetration of the vagina or anus with a body part (other than a penis — that would be rape) or an object, without consent. This includes fingers, tongue, or any object. It is as serious as rape in law and carries the same maximum sentence.

### Points to Prove
1. The suspect intentionally penetrated the vagina or anus of another person with a part of their body or anything else
2. The penetration was sexual
3. The complainant did not consent
4. The suspect did not reasonably believe the complainant consented

### Maximum Sentence: Life imprisonment

## SEXUAL ASSAULT — Sexual Offences Act 2003, Section 3

### Plain English
Sexual assault is any intentional sexual touching of your body without your consent. This includes:
- Grabbing or groping any part of your body
- Rubbing against you sexually in a crowd
- Kissing you without consent
- Forcing you to touch them sexually
- Touching over clothing

It does NOT require penetration. It does NOT require violence. Any unwanted sexual touch counts.

### Points to Prove
1. The suspect intentionally touched another person
2. The touching was sexual
3. The complainant did not consent to the touching
4. The suspect did not reasonably believe the complainant consented

### Maximum Sentence: 10 years imprisonment

## SEXUAL HARASSMENT — Criminal Justice Act 2023 / Equality Act 2010

### Plain English
Sexual harassment is unwanted behaviour of a sexual nature. It can happen in the street, in a bar, online, or at work. Since the Criminal Justice Act 2023, street harassment is a specific criminal offence in England & Wales. You don't have to have said "stop" for something to be harassment — if it was unwanted and sexual, it qualifies.

### Street Sexual Harassment — Points to Prove
1. The suspect engaged in behaviour that was unwanted
2. The behaviour was of a sexual nature
3. It occurred in a public space
4. The victim was caused alarm, distress or humiliation (or this was intended)

### Workplace Harassment (Equality Act 2010)
Your employer has a legal duty to protect you from sexual harassment at work. The Worker Protection Act 2023 strengthened this duty — employers must now take "reasonable steps" to prevent harassment. You can bring a claim at an Employment Tribunal.

### Examples
- Catcalling, wolf-whistling, sexual comments in the street
- Following while making sexual comments
- Unwanted sexual comments or touching at work
- Sending unsolicited sexual images ("dick pics" — this can also be an offence under s.66A SOA 2003)
- Sexual comments or messages on social media

## VOYEURISM & UPSKIRTING — SOA 2003 s.67 / Voyeurism (Offences) Act 2019

### Plain English
Voyeurism is when someone secretly watches you doing something private for their own sexual gratification. Upskirting — taking a photo or video up someone's skirt or kilt without permission — became a specific criminal offence in April 2019 following campaigning by Gina Martin. Hidden cameras in bathrooms, changing rooms, or bedrooms are also covered.

### Upskirting — Points to Prove
1. The suspect operated equipment beneath a person's clothing
2. Without that person's consent
3. To observe genitalia or buttocks, OR to obtain sexual gratification, OR to humiliate/distress the victim

### Voyeurism (s.67) — Points to Prove
1. The suspect observed another person for the purpose of sexual gratification
2. The other person was doing a private act
3. The suspect knew the other person did not consent to being observed

### Hidden Cameras — What to Do
If you discover a hidden camera:
1. Do NOT touch or move it
2. Photograph it in situ (without touching)
3. Call police immediately
4. Note the exact location and time

### Maximum Sentence
- Upskirting: up to 2 years (up to 5 if placed on Sex Offenders Register)
- Voyeurism: up to 2 years

## CONTROLLING AND COERCIVE BEHAVIOUR — Serious Crime Act 2015, Section 76

### Plain English
Coercive control is when a partner or ex uses a **pattern of behaviour** to trap you, scare you, control what you do, where you go, and who you see. It's often invisible to the outside world. It can exist without physical violence. Many victims don't recognise it as a crime because it builds up gradually — the "boiling frog" effect. If you feel like you're constantly walking on eggshells, that matters.

### Points to Prove
1. The suspect and victim are personally connected (intimate partners/ex-partners, or family members sharing a home)
2. The suspect engaged in **repeated or continuous** controlling or coercive behaviour
3. The behaviour had a **serious effect** on the victim — either:
   - Causing the victim to fear violence will be used against them, OR
   - Causing serious alarm or distress that has a substantial adverse effect on their day-to-day activities
4. The suspect knew or ought to have known the behaviour would have this effect

### Signs — Is This Happening to You?
- Checking your phone, emails, or social media without permission
- Controlling money — stopping you working, giving you an "allowance," demanding receipts
- Isolating you from friends and family
- Constantly criticising or humiliating you
- Making all decisions for the family without consulting you
- Monitoring your location using apps
- Threatening to share intimate images or private information
- Making you feel like you are "going mad" — denying things they said or did (gaslighting)
- Using children as a weapon — threats about custody or social services
- Controlling what you eat, wear, or how you present yourself
- Threatening to harm themselves if you leave

### Maximum Sentence: 5 years imprisonment (increased from 2015 by Domestic Abuse Act 2021)

### Note on the Domestic Abuse Act 2021
The Act created a statutory definition of domestic abuse that includes psychological, emotional, economic and coercive abuse. It also extended coercive control to former partners who no longer share a home.

## REVENGE PORN (Non-Consensual Intimate Images) — Online Safety Act 2023

### Plain English
Revenge porn is when someone shares — or threatens to share — intimate photos or videos of you without your permission. The term "revenge porn" is misleading because:
- The images don't have to be sexual (just intimate)
- You don't have to have shared them originally for it to be a crime
- The motivation doesn't have to be "revenge" — it's always illegal without consent

The Online Safety Act 2023 significantly strengthened the law. Even **threatening to share** images is now a criminal offence. Sharing images to cause distress carries a higher sentence.

### Points to Prove
1. A photograph or film was shared (or threatened to be shared)
2. It showed private sexual activity or genitalia
3. The victim did not consent to the sharing
4. The suspect intended to cause distress (or was reckless about causing it)

### What To Do Immediately
1. **Do not delete evidence** — screenshot the post, the URL, the account
2. Report to police with your evidence
3. Contact the **Revenge Porn Helpline** (0345 6000 459) — they remove images from platforms
4. Go to **StopNCII.org** to create a digital fingerprint to prevent future uploads
5. Report to the platform directly
6. Consider contacting a solicitor for an emergency injunction

### Maximum Sentence
- Sharing intimate images: up to 2 years
- Sharing with intent to cause distress: up to 2 years
- Threatening to share: up to 2 years

## INTIMATE AI DEEPFAKES — Online Safety Act 2023 / Criminal Justice Bill 2024

### Plain English
A deepfake is a realistic fake image or video created using AI that puts your face onto another body — often in sexual situations. This can be done using only ordinary photos taken from your social media, without you ever posing for any sexual image.

Sharing intimate deepfakes without consent is a criminal offence under the Online Safety Act 2023. The Criminal Justice Bill 2024 goes further: it will criminalise the **creation** of intimate deepfakes, even if they are never shared.

### Points to Prove (Sharing — OSA 2023)
1. A deepfake intimate image was shared
2. The victim did not consent
3. The suspect had no reasonable belief the victim consented

### What to Do
1. Screenshot the image, the URL, the account — preserve evidence first
2. Report to police
3. Report to the platform — request emergency removal
4. Contact the Revenge Porn Helpline — they assist with deepfakes
5. Contact a solicitor for an emergency injunction if the image is spreading

### Note on Evolving Law
This area of law is developing rapidly. The government has committed to criminalising creation. Always check current guidance at legislation.gov.uk or with a specialist solicitor.
